# Transaction Analysis

## 1. Review Scope

This analysis reviews the fictional customer’s account activity for the period 2 May 2026 to 30 May 2026.

The purpose is to compare the actual transactions with the expected customer profile established during onboarding and identify activity requiring clarification or escalation.

## 2. Expected Customer Activity

At onboarding, the customer stated that:

- Monthly turnover would range from N$80,000 to N$120,000
- Incoming funds would mainly consist of retail-business revenue
- Outgoing funds would mainly consist of payments to South African suppliers
- Activity would primarily involve Namibia and South Africa
- Transactions would be consistent with the operation of a retail business

## 3. Transaction Summary

A total of 20 transactions were reviewed.

| Category | Number of Transactions | Total Amount |
|---|---:|---:|
| Incoming | 9 | N$218,550 |
| Outgoing | 11 | N$247,000 |
| Total account activity | 20 | N$465,550 |
| Net movement | — | N$28,450 net outflow |

The total incoming activity of N$218,550 exceeded the customer’s stated maximum expected monthly turnover of N$120,000 by N$98,550.

This represents approximately 82% more incoming activity than the upper end of the expected range.

## 4. Activity Consistent with the Customer Profile

Several transactions appear consistent with the stated retail-business purpose.

## 5. Activity Outside the Expected Profile

The remaining activity requires further review because it differs from the customer’s stated purpose or expected transaction behaviour.

### 5.1 Third-Party Transfer Followed by Rapid Outgoing Payment

On 6 May 2026:

- TX004 recorded an incoming payment of N$30,000 from K. Shikongo, described as a loan
- TX005 recorded an outgoing payment of N$29,500 to Africa Trade Solutions on the same day, described as a consulting fee

The outgoing payment represented approximately 98% of the incoming transfer.

The same-day movement of nearly the full amount may indicate pass-through activity and is not clearly connected to the customer’s stated retail operations.

**Required clarification:**

- Identity and relationship of K. Shikongo
- Purpose and terms of the alleged loan
- Reason for the consulting payment
- Nature of Africa Trade Solutions
- Supporting loan agreement, invoice and proof of services

### 5.2 Family Transfer Followed by Digital-Asset Payment

On 14 May 2026:

- TX009 recorded an incoming payment of N$50,000 from M. Amutenya, described as family support
- TX010 recorded an outgoing payment of N$50,000 to Global Digital Assets in the United Arab Emirates on the same day

The full amount received was transferred out on the same day.

The transaction involved:

- A third-party personal payment
- Immediate movement of the full amount
- A digital-asset-related counterparty
- A jurisdiction outside the expected Namibia–South Africa profile
- An investment purpose unrelated to retail supplier payments

This activity is materially inconsistent with the customer’s stated account purpose.

**Required clarification:**

- Identity and relationship of M. Amutenya
- Purpose of the family-support payment
- Reason for using the business account
- Nature of the digital-asset investment
- Ownership and regulatory status of the recipient
- Supporting evidence for the source and destination of funds

### 5.3 Repeated Round-Number Payments to Individuals

On 18 May 2026, three payments of N$10,000 each were made to three different individuals:

- TX012: T. Ndeitunga
- TX013: P. Hamukwaya
- TX014: L. Nghipandulwa

Each payment used the general reference “Business services”.

The pattern is unusual because:

- All three payments occurred on the same day
- Each payment was for the same round-number amount
- The recipients were individuals rather than known suppliers
- The payment descriptions were vague
- No supporting commercial purpose was recorded

The activity may reflect legitimate service payments, but the repeated structure and lack of detail require investigation.

**Required clarification:**

- Identity and role of each recipient
- Nature of the services provided
- Contracts, invoices or proof of work
- Reason for identical payment amounts
- Whether the recipients are connected to the customer

### 5.4 Unexpected Foreign Payment Chain

On 25 May 2026:

- TX017 recorded an incoming payment of N$42,000 from Blue Rock Trading in Botswana, described as a settlement
- TX018 recorded an outgoing payment of N$40,000 to North Star Holdings in Mauritius on the same day, described as consultancy

Approximately 95% of the incoming amount was transferred out on the same day.

The activity is outside the expected profile because:

- Neither Botswana nor Mauritius was identified as an expected jurisdiction
- The counterparties were not disclosed during onboarding
- The references “settlement” and “consultancy” are vague
- The activity does not clearly relate to retail stock purchases
- The funds were moved rapidly across borders

**Required clarification:**

- Nature of the relationship with both entities
- Purpose of the settlement
- Details of the consultancy arrangement
- Contracts, invoices and correspondence
- Reason for routing the funds through the customer’s account
- Beneficial ownership and business activities of the counterparties

## 6. Pass-Through Activity

Three transaction sequences display possible pass-through characteristics:

| Incoming Transaction | Outgoing Transaction | Incoming Amount | Outgoing Amount | Timing |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| TX004 | TX005 | N$30,000 | N$29,500 | Same day |
| TX009 | TX010 | N$50,000 | N$50,000 | Same day |
| TX017 | TX018 | N$42,000 | N$40,000 | Same day |

A combined N$122,000 was received through these three transactions.

A combined N$119,500 was then transferred out on the same respective days.

Approximately 98% of the incoming funds in these sequences were rapidly moved onward.

This may indicate that the account is being used as a temporary conduit rather than solely for the customer’s stated retail operations.

## 7. Geographic Analysis

Expected jurisdictions were Namibia and South Africa.

Actual activity also involved:

- Botswana
- Mauritius
- United Arab Emirates

The additional jurisdictions were not disclosed in the original customer profile.

The geographic expansion, particularly when combined with rapid fund movement and unclear transaction purposes, increases the level of risk.

## 8. Turnover Comparison

The customer expected monthly turnover of N$80,000 to N$120,000.

The account received N$218,550 during the review period.

However, only N$96,550 was described as retail receipts. The remaining N$122,000 consisted of:

- A personal loan
- Family support
- A foreign settlement

The additional receipts caused the account activity to exceed the expected profile and were not clearly connected to ordinary retail sales.

## 9. Key Findings

The analysis identified the following concerns:

- Incoming activity substantially exceeded the expected monthly range
- N$122,000 of incoming funds did not represent stated retail revenue
- Three instances of possible same-day pass-through activity occurred
- Large third-party payments were received
- Repeated round-number payments were made to individuals
- Vague references such as “business services”, “settlement” and “consultancy” were used
- Unexpected jurisdictions were involved
- A payment was made to a digital-asset-related counterparty
- Several transactions were inconsistent with the stated account purpose
- Supporting documents are required to establish a legitimate commercial explanation

## 10. Preliminary Assessment

The normal retail receipts and identifiable supplier payments appear broadly consistent with the customer profile.

However, the unusual activity is significant in value, frequency and pattern. It cannot be reasonably explained using the information currently available.

The activity should not automatically be treated as criminal. Nevertheless, it warrants further investigation and customer clarification.

## 11. Recommended Next Steps

The following actions are recommended:

1. Place the case under enhanced review
2. Request supporting documents for the flagged transactions
3. Verify the identities and relationships of the third-party senders and recipients
4. Review the counterparties using sanctions, PEP and adverse-media screening
5. Establish the commercial purpose of the Botswana, Mauritius and UAE transactions
6. Obtain evidence supporting the loan, family transfer, settlement, consultancy and service payments
7. Consider whether the account is being used for activity unrelated to the stated business
8. Reassess the customer’s risk rating
9. Escalate the matter to the appropriate AML or compliance function
10. Continue monitoring for similar transaction patterns

## Preliminary Conclusion

The review identified multiple material deviations from the expected customer profile.

The combined presence of rapid fund movement, third-party transfers, repeated round-number payments, unexpected jurisdictions and unclear transaction purposes justifies escalation for further investigation.
