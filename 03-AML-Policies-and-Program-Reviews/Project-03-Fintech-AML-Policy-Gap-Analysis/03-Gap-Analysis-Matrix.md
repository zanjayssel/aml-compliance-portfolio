# AML Policy Gap Analysis Matrix

## 1. Purpose

This matrix evaluates the fictional NorthBridge Pay, Inc. AML policy against selected U.S. BSA/AML, FinCEN money-services-business and OFAC compliance principles.

The review focuses on whether the policy provides sufficiently clear, risk-based and operationally usable controls.

## 2. Assessment Method

Each policy area is assigned:

- A current-control assessment
- A gap description
- A risk rating
- A recommended remediation action

## 3. Risk Rating Scale

| Rating | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Low | Minor drafting or administrative weakness with limited immediate compliance impact |
| Moderate | Control weakness that may result in inconsistent execution or incomplete records |
| High | Material weakness that may prevent effective identification, management or escalation of AML risk |
| Critical | Fundamental program weakness requiring urgent senior-management attention |

## 4. Gap Analysis Matrix

| No. | Policy Area | Current Policy Position | Identified Gap | Risk Rating | Recommended Remediation |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | AML governance and oversight | The Chief Compliance Officer oversees the AML program and may delegate tasks. | The policy does not define the CCO’s authority, access to information, reporting obligations, resources or independence. Board and senior-management oversight responsibilities are also unclear. | High | Define the CCO’s responsibilities and authority, establish formal senior-management and board reporting, and require documented review of material AML issues. |
| 2 | Enterprise AML risk assessment | The policy contains general references to customer and transaction risk. | There is no documented assessment of the company’s products, services, customers, delivery channels, geographic exposure or crypto-related risk. | Critical | Require a documented enterprise-wide AML risk assessment that is reviewed periodically and whenever material business changes occur. |
| 3 | Customer identification and verification | Basic identifying information is collected and third-party verification may be used. | The policy does not define acceptable documents, verification standards, failed or inconclusive verification procedures, exception handling or account-opening restrictions. | High | Establish clear identity-verification procedures, acceptable evidence, escalation requirements and rules for failed or incomplete verification. |
| 4 | Customer Due Diligence | The policy states that customer identity, business purpose, ownership and expected activity may be considered. | There is no mandatory CDD standard, documented customer-acceptance process or requirement to establish a complete expected activity profile. | High | Introduce minimum CDD requirements for every customer and require documented approval before account activation. |
| 5 | Customer risk rating | The policy lists possible risk factors. | There is no scoring methodology, weighting system, risk-rating scale, approval process or record of how the final rating is reached. | Critical | Implement a documented customer-risk methodology covering customer, product, geographic, transaction and delivery-channel risk. |
| 6 | Enhanced Due Diligence | The policy states that EDD may be performed where appropriate. | EDD triggers, required evidence, decision-makers, review frequency and exit criteria are undefined. | High | Define mandatory EDD triggers, required checks, source-of-funds and source-of-wealth expectations, senior approval and enhanced-review cycles. |
| 7 | Beneficial ownership | Business customers provide ownership information. | The policy does not define ownership thresholds, indirect ownership, control through other means, verification procedures or treatment of complex structures. | High | Create documented procedures to identify, verify and record relevant natural-person owners and controlling individuals using a risk-based approach. |
| 8 | Ongoing customer review | The policy contains no detailed periodic-review process. | There is no schedule for reviewing low-, medium- or high-risk customers and no event-driven review criteria. | High | Establish risk-based review intervals and trigger events such as ownership changes, unusual activity, adverse media or material changes in customer behaviour. |
| 9 | Sanctions screening | Customers are screened through a third-party provider and potential matches are referred to the CCO. | Screening frequency, list coverage, transaction screening, alert resolution, false-positive documentation and escalation procedures are not defined. | Critical | Introduce a documented risk-based sanctions compliance process covering onboarding, ongoing screening, payment screening, alert review, escalation and recordkeeping. |
| 10 | PEP screening | PEPs may receive additional due diligence and management review. | The policy does not define domestic or foreign PEPs, family members, close associates, approval requirements, source-of-wealth review or ongoing monitoring. | High | Define PEP categories and require appropriate EDD, senior approval, source-of-wealth review and ongoing monitoring. |
| 11 | Transaction monitoring | The company uses automated and manual monitoring and lists examples of unusual activity. | The policy does not document monitoring rules, thresholds, risk coverage, alert handling, investigation standards, tuning or validation. | Critical | Document the transaction-monitoring framework, rule rationale, investigation procedures, escalation standards, quality controls and periodic effectiveness reviews. |
| 12 | Crypto-related monitoring | The policy briefly refers to repeated crypto-related transactions. | There is no specific treatment of wallet risk, blockchain exposure, mixers, high-risk exchanges, rapid fiat-to-crypto movement or crypto counterparty screening. | High | Develop proportionate crypto-specific controls, including enhanced monitoring, counterparty review and escalation criteria. |
| 13 | Suspicious-activity investigation | Employees report concerns to the CCO, who decides whether further investigation or reporting is required. | The policy lacks investigation stages, ownership, timelines, documentation standards, decision criteria and case-closure requirements. | Critical | Establish a formal investigation and escalation procedure with defined roles, timelines, evidence requirements and decision records. |
| 14 | SAR decision and filing governance | The policy refers generally to reporting suspicious activity. | It does not explain who may approve a SAR decision, how decisions are documented, applicable deadlines, confidentiality, continuing activity or supporting-record retention. | Critical | Develop a documented SAR governance procedure for applicable activity, including decision authority, filing controls, confidentiality and retained evidence. |
| 15 | Recordkeeping and retention | Records are retained for an “appropriate period.” | No specific retention periods, ownership, access controls, file standards or destruction requirements are stated. | High | Create a formal retention schedule and require complete, secure and retrievable customer, screening, monitoring, investigation, reporting and training records. |
| 16 | Employee training | Employees receive AML training during onboarding and additional training may be provided. | Annual, role-specific and management training are not mandatory. Completion, testing and remediation are not documented. | High | Establish mandatory onboarding and recurring training, tailored by role, with completion records, knowledge testing and follow-up training. |
| 17 | Independent review | The company may arrange a review when considered appropriate. | Independent testing is presented as optional. Frequency, scope, reviewer independence, reporting and remediation tracking are undefined. | Critical | Require periodic independent testing by a suitably knowledgeable and independent reviewer, with findings reported to management and formally remediated. |
| 18 | Quality assurance | No structured quality-assurance process is described. | The company has no documented review of KYC files, monitoring alerts, investigations or screening dispositions. | High | Implement risk-based quality assurance with sample testing, error classification, root-cause analysis and corrective-action tracking. |
| 19 | Third-party vendor oversight | The company states that it will use reputable providers and that compliance and operations share responsibility. | Due diligence, contracting standards, service-level monitoring, data quality, testing, incident escalation and exit planning are absent. | High | Establish a vendor-governance framework covering selection, due diligence, contractual requirements, monitoring, testing and contingency planning. |
| 20 | Management information and reporting | The board receives limited compliance updates. | There is no defined reporting frequency or set of key compliance metrics. | High | Require regular management information covering onboarding, customer risk, screening alerts, transaction-monitoring cases, SAR decisions, training, testing and remediation. |
| 21 | Policy approval and review | The policy is reviewed “periodically” and may be approved by the CCO or senior management. | The review frequency, formal approval authority, version control and trigger events are unclear. | Moderate | Require at least annual review, documented approval by an appropriate governing authority, version control and event-driven updates. |
| 22 | Regulatory change management | The policy contains no regulatory-change process. | There is no method for identifying, assessing and implementing changes in applicable requirements or guidance. | High | Establish a regulatory-change process with assigned ownership, impact assessment, implementation tracking and management reporting. |
| 23 | Issue and remediation tracking | Identified weaknesses are reported to management. | The policy does not require action owners, deadlines, severity classifications, evidence of completion or validation. | High | Create a central remediation log with accountable owners, target dates, status reporting and independent closure validation. |
| 24 | Confidentiality and information security | The policy states that records must be stored securely. | Access controls, authorised users, secure transfer, confidentiality requirements and incident escalation are not adequately described. | Moderate | Define access restrictions, secure storage and transmission requirements, confidentiality obligations and incident-reporting procedures. |

## 5. Summary of Findings

The review identified:

| High | 14 |
| Moderate | 3 |
| Low | 0 |
| **Total** | **24** |

## 6. Critical Gaps

The most urgent weaknesses are:

1. Absence of an enterprise AML risk assessment
2. Absence of a documented customer-risk-rating methodology
3. Inadequate sanctions-screening procedures
4. Incomplete transaction-monitoring governance
5. Inadequate suspicious-activity investigation procedures
6. Incomplete SAR decision and filing governance
7. Optional and undefined independent testing

## 7. Overall Assessment

The existing AML policy acknowledges many relevant compliance topics, but it does not convert them into sufficiently clear and operational controls.

Frequent use of wording such as “may”, “where necessary”, “periodically” and “when appropriate” allows inconsistent application and provides limited evidence of a structured risk-based program.

The policy is therefore not yet suitable for presentation as a mature AML control framework to a sponsor bank or payment-processing partner.

## 8. Overall Risk Conclusion

**Overall Program Risk:** High

**Policy Readiness:** Material Remediation Required

**Recommended Management Response:** Prioritised remediation plan followed by formal approval, implementation, staff training and independent testing.
