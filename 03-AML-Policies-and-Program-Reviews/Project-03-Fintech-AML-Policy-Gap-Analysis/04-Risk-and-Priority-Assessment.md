# Risk and Priority Assessment

## 1. Purpose

This assessment prioritises the weaknesses identified in the NorthBridge Pay, Inc. AML Policy Gap Analysis.

The objective is to help management determine:

- Which weaknesses require immediate action
- Which issues present the greatest financial-crime and regulatory exposure
- Which remediation actions should be completed before the policy is presented to the sponsor bank
- Which improvements may be implemented through a phased remediation plan

## 2. Prioritisation Method

Each gap is assessed using four factors:

1. **Regulatory significance**  
   The extent to which the weakness affects a fundamental AML or sanctions-control expectation.

2. **Financial-crime exposure**  
   The likelihood that the weakness may allow suspicious, prohibited or unexplained activity to go undetected.

3. **Operational impact**  
   The extent to which the weakness may cause inconsistent decisions, incomplete records or ineffective controls.

4. **External stakeholder impact**  
   The likelihood that the weakness may concern the sponsor bank, payment processor, investor or external reviewer.

## 3. Priority Levels

| Priority | Meaning | Suggested Response |
|---|---|---|
| Priority 1 — Immediate | Fundamental weakness creating material programme or financial-crime risk | Begin remediation immediately and complete before sponsor-bank submission where possible |
| Priority 2 — High | Significant control weakness requiring prompt correction | Assign an owner and complete within 30 to 60 days |
| Priority 3 — Medium | Important weakness affecting consistency, governance or documentation | Complete within 60 to 90 days |
| Priority 4 — Lower | Administrative or drafting improvement with limited immediate risk | Include in the next policy-review cycle |

## 4. Priority 1 — Immediate Remediation

### 4.1 Enterprise AML Risk Assessment

**Gap:** NorthBridge Pay has not completed a documented enterprise-wide AML risk assessment.

**Why it matters:**  
Without a formal risk assessment, the company cannot demonstrate that its controls are reasonably designed around its products, customers, jurisdictions, payment channels and crypto-related exposure.

**Potential impact:**

- Controls may not address the company’s highest-risk activities
- Monitoring rules may not reflect actual risk
- High-risk products or jurisdictions may receive insufficient scrutiny
- Sponsor-bank confidence may be reduced

**Required action:**

- Complete an enterprise AML risk assessment
- Assess customer, product, geographic, transaction and delivery-channel risk
- Document inherent risk, existing controls and residual risk
- Obtain senior-management approval
- Review the assessment at least annually and after material business changes

**Priority:** Immediate

---

### 4.2 Customer Risk-Rating Methodology

**Gap:** No documented customer-risk scoring model exists.

**Why it matters:**  
The company cannot consistently determine which customers require standard due diligence, enhanced due diligence, senior approval or more frequent review.

**Potential impact:**

- High-risk customers may be incorrectly classified
- EDD may be applied inconsistently
- Monitoring intensity may not reflect customer risk
- Risk decisions may be difficult to defend

**Required action:**

- Establish a customer-risk-rating methodology
- Define low-, medium- and high-risk categories
- Identify and weight relevant risk factors
- Require documented rationale for the final rating
- Define approval and review requirements for each category

**Priority:** Immediate

---

### 4.3 Sanctions-Screening Framework

**Gap:** Sanctions screening is referenced but not supported by documented operating procedures.

**Why it matters:**  
The policy does not define screening timing, list coverage, alert investigation, escalation, false-positive resolution or recordkeeping.

**Potential impact:**

- Potential sanctions matches may not be identified or escalated correctly
- Payments may be processed before screening is completed
- False positives may be closed without sufficient evidence
- The company may be unable to demonstrate effective sanctions controls

**Required action:**

- Document onboarding, ongoing and transaction-screening requirements
- Define alert review and escalation procedures
- Record false-positive resolution
- Establish procedures for possible true matches
- Test the effectiveness of the screening provider

**Priority:** Immediate

---

### 4.4 Transaction-Monitoring Governance

**Gap:** The company uses monitoring rules but has not documented their rationale, coverage or investigation standards.

**Why it matters:**  
A monitoring system cannot be assessed effectively if management cannot explain what risks each rule addresses and how alerts are reviewed.

**Potential impact:**

- Relevant suspicious activity may not generate alerts
- Excessive false positives may consume analyst resources
- Investigations may be inconsistent
- Monitoring effectiveness may not be demonstrated to the sponsor bank

**Required action:**

- Document all monitoring rules and thresholds
- Link each rule to identified risks and typologies
- Define alert-investigation procedures
- Establish escalation and closure standards
- Introduce periodic rule tuning, validation and quality assurance

**Priority:** Immediate

---

### 4.5 Suspicious-Activity Investigation and Escalation

**Gap:** There is no formal investigation workflow or documented escalation standard.

**Why it matters:**  
Analysts may apply different standards, omit relevant evidence or fail to escalate material concerns within an appropriate timeframe.

**Potential impact:**

- Suspicious activity may not be investigated adequately
- Case decisions may be inconsistent
- Escalations may be delayed
- Supporting records may be incomplete

**Required action:**

- Create a formal investigation procedure
- Define roles, stages and timelines
- Standardise case notes and narratives
- Establish evidence and escalation requirements
- Require documented reasons for escalation and closure decisions

**Priority:** Immediate

---

### 4.6 SAR Decision and Filing Governance

**Gap:** The policy does not define the process for evaluating and documenting whether external suspicious-activity reporting is required.

**Why it matters:**  
Certain MSBs, including money transmitters, are subject to suspicious-activity reporting requirements. A documented governance process is therefore necessary where the business model falls within an applicable reporting category. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

**Potential impact:**

- Reporting decisions may be delayed or inconsistent
- Filing deadlines may be missed
- Confidentiality may be compromised
- Supporting records may not be retained
- Decisions not to report may lack documented reasoning

**Required action:**

- Define reporting-decision authority
- Document review and approval requirements
- Establish filing and escalation timelines
- Protect SAR confidentiality
- Retain supporting evidence and decision records
- Establish continuing-activity review procedures where applicable

**Priority:** Immediate

---

### 4.7 Independent Testing

**Gap:** Independent testing is described as optional and has no defined schedule or scope.

**Why it matters:**  
Independent review is used to evaluate whether an MSB’s AML programme is adequate, complies with applicable BSA obligations and is operating consistently with its own policies and procedures. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

**Potential impact:**

- Control failures may remain unidentified
- Management may rely on ineffective processes
- Remediation may not be independently validated
- External stakeholders may question programme maturity

**Required action:**

- Establish a periodic independent-review schedule
- Define reviewer independence and competence
- Include testing of policies, procedures and operational effectiveness
- Report findings to senior management
- Track and validate remediation

**Priority:** Immediate

## 6. Priority 3 — Medium-Priority Remediation

The following areas remain important but may be completed after the core control weaknesses have been addressed:

### Policy Approval and Version Control

The company should establish:

- A fixed annual review cycle
- Formal approval authority
- Version numbering
- Effective dates
- Change logs
- Event-driven review triggers

### Confidentiality and Information Handling

The company should define:

- Role-based access
- Secure storage
- Secure transfer of compliance information
- Confidentiality responsibilities
- Incident-escalation procedures
- Restrictions on access to sensitive investigation and reporting records

## 9. Overall Priority Conclusion

NorthBridge Pay should not treat the identified weaknesses as isolated drafting defects.

The most serious gaps affect the core design and operation of the AML programme, including risk assessment, customer classification, sanctions controls, transaction monitoring, investigations, reporting governance and independent testing.

The company should complete the Priority 1 remediation actions before presenting the policy as a mature control framework to its sponsor bank.
